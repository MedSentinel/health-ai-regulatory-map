# Claim Language

Version: `v0.1`

This page is about language that sounds reassuring, mature, or clinically serious, but often needs careful unpacking.

It is not arguing that these phrases are always misleading.
It is arguing that they should almost never be read at face value.

## Why this page exists

In health AI, a large part of the confusion happens before any regulatory framework is even discussed.

It happens at the language level.

Words like:

- patient-centered
- clinically proven
- decision support
- FDA-cleared
- safe
- responsible
- real-world validated

can signal something real.
They can also compress a lot of unresolved uncertainty into a polished phrase.

## Common phrases and what to ask

### "Patient-centered"

Possible meaning:

- designed to reduce burden for the patient
- designed to improve the patient experience
- designed for patients as users
- designed mainly for clinician efficiency but rhetorically linked to patient benefit

Questions to ask:

- Who is the direct user?
- What exactly improves for the patient?
- Is there visible outcome or experience evidence?
- Is workflow efficiency being rebranded as patient benefit?

### "Clinically proven"

Possible meaning:

- tested in a narrow study
- externally validated in some form
- associated with a published paper
- marketed more strongly than the visible evidence supports

Questions to ask:

- Proven in what setting?
- Against what comparator?
- For what endpoint?
- Prospective, retrospective, or benchmark only?

### "Decision support only"

Possible meaning:

- the company wants to signal that the clinician remains in charge
- the product may still meaningfully shape the decision pathway

Questions to ask:

- What does the system rank, filter, suggest, or prioritize?
- Does it influence timing, escalation, or attention?
- Is the human review meaningful or mostly nominal?

### "Not intended to diagnose"

Possible meaning:

- the product is framed narrowly for regulatory comfort
- the real-world user experience may still feel diagnostic

Questions to ask:

- What does the product actually cause users to do?
- Does it steer patient-specific action?
- Does it narrow attention toward a clinical conclusion anyway?

### "FDA-cleared"

Possible meaning:

- some part of the product or use case has a defined regulatory status
- the phrase is being used too broadly across adjacent capabilities

Questions to ask:

- Cleared for exactly what intended use?
- Which version or module?
- Is the public language broader than the cleared scope?

### "Real-world validated"

Possible meaning:

- tested in an operational environment
- piloted under limited conditions
- rhetorically upgraded from narrow deployment evidence

Questions to ask:

- How many sites?
- What kind of users?
- What duration?
- What failure modes were observed?

### "Responsible AI" or "governed AI"

Possible meaning:

- serious internal governance exists
- governance language is compensating for thin product evidence

Questions to ask:

- What concrete governance mechanisms are visible?
- Is governance language more specific than validation language?
- Is the product evidence as mature as the trust language?

## Practical reading rule

When a phrase sounds mature, ask:

1. What narrower claim could this phrase honestly mean?
2. What stronger interpretation is the reader likely to hear?
3. What evidence would be needed to justify that stronger interpretation?

That gap is often where the real editorial story lives.

## Common editorial outcomes

- The phrase is directionally fair, but underspecified.
- The phrase is stronger than the visible evidence.
- The phrase compresses several different ideas into one reassuring label.
- The phrase shifts attention away from the actual unresolved risk.
