# Review Questions

Version: `v0.1`

This page is a practical editorial aid.

It is designed for reviewing:

- product launches
- company announcements
- papers
- demos
- founder claims
- media coverage

It is not a compliance checklist.

## Core questions

### 1. What is the product actually claiming to do?

- Is the claim about diagnosis, screening, triage, monitoring, documentation, or workflow?
- Is the public language narrower or broader than the likely practical effect?

### 2. Who is the user?

- clinician
- patient
- consumer
- operator
- health system buyer

The same product can raise different questions depending on who is expected to rely on it.

### 3. What is the intended use?

- Is the intended use explicit, or implied through marketing language?
- Is the product being positioned as support, assistance, prediction, or action guidance?

### 4. What evidence is actually visible?

- prospective evidence
- retrospective evidence
- benchmark only
- no visible evidence

### 5. What harms or burdens might the system create?

- false positives
- false reassurance
- workflow friction
- alert fatigue
- follow-up burden
- incidental findings
- operator over-reliance

### 6. What kind of human oversight still exists?

- meaningful review
- nominal review
- no clear interruption point

### 7. What language should trigger extra caution?

- patient-centered
- safer
- more accurate
- faster diagnosis
- real-world validated
- clinically proven
- FDA-cleared

These phrases are not meaningless, but they often need unpacking.

## Fast red flags

If several of the following appear together, slow down:

- broad claims with narrow evidence
- safety language without failure discussion
- replacement framing without validation detail
- regulatory references without intended-use clarity
- workflow benefit claims without burden analysis
- governance language standing in for product evidence

## Fast review outcome labels

These labels are useful for editorial triage:

### Plausible but under-evidenced

Use when the idea may be real, but the visible proof is thin.

### Useful-looking but boundary-unclear

Use when the tool may help, but support and influence are not clearly separated.

### Strong framing, weak evidence

Use when the rhetoric outruns the visible methods or validation.

### Governance-heavy, product-light

Use when a company sounds serious but reveals little about real product performance.

### Worth tracking, not yet trusting

Use when the direction matters, but the evidence base is not mature enough for confidence.

## Review outcome

After asking the questions above, you should usually be able to say one of the following:

- the claim looks directionally plausible but under-evidenced
- the framing is stronger than the visible evidence
- the product may be useful, but the safety and governance picture is still unclear
- the regulatory language is being used too loosely
- the main issue is not model performance but workflow or downstream burden
