# FDA

Version: `v0.1`

## Why the FDA matters here

For many health AI products, the FDA lens becomes important when software starts making or shaping claims that affect diagnosis, treatment, screening, triage, or patient management.

The most important question is often not "does this use AI?"

It is:

What is the product claiming to do, for whom, and in what context?

## What this document is for

This is not a full FDA guide.

It is a public-facing orientation note for understanding the kinds of questions the FDA lens introduces for health AI.

## Key FDA-oriented questions

1. What is the intended use?
2. Is the software making a medical claim, or only supporting general information handling?
3. Is it patient-facing, clinician-facing, or both?
4. Is it informing care, shaping action, or triggering downstream decisions?
5. How much human oversight is still meaningful?
6. What evidence exists for clinical performance?
7. Are marketing claims broader than the likely validated use?

## Why this matters for MedSentinel

Many health AI discussions use FDA language casually.

But the hard part is rarely the presence of AI alone.
The hard part is the combination of:

- intended use
- user type
- decision consequence
- evidence standard
- degree of autonomy or influence

## Common mistake

Treating "not a final diagnosis tool" as if it automatically resolves the regulatory question.

In practice, systems can still shape care meaningfully without issuing a final diagnosis.

## What to look for in public claims

- screening language
- triage language
- decision support framing
- clinician efficiency claims that drift toward clinical influence
- patient reassurance or action guidance
