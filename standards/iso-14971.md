# ISO 14971

Version: `v0.1`

## Why this matters

ISO 14971 is one of the most important reference points when people discuss medical-device style risk management.

For MedSentinel purposes, the key point is not the full standard text.

It is the habit of thinking it represents:

- identify hazards
- assess risk
- mitigate risk
- monitor residual risk over time

## Why it matters for health AI

Health AI systems often create risks that are not limited to obvious software bugs.

Examples include:

- false reassurance
- false positives
- workflow distortion
- over-reliance
- hidden clinical influence
- failure to escalate

Risk management language is useful because it forces attention onto consequence, not just capability.

## What to look for in public materials

You usually will not see a full risk file.
But stronger public materials often signal that the team has thought about:

- failure modes
- false reassurance
- user over-reliance
- mitigation steps
- known limitations
- monitoring after deployment

## Common mistake

Treating a reference to ISO 14971 as if it automatically proves a system is safe.

Referencing a standard is not the same thing as showing how risk was actually understood, mitigated, and monitored.

## What this standard does not tell you by itself

Even if a company references ISO 14971, you still do not know:

- whether the evidence is clinically strong
- whether the workflow is realistic
- whether subgroup performance is acceptable
- whether the mitigations hold up in practice

## Good editorial questions

1. What are the product’s plausible harm pathways?
2. Are the harms only technical, or also workflow and interpretation related?
3. What residual risks remain after mitigation?
4. Is the system likely to create downstream burden even when it performs as intended?

## Especially relevant for

- imaging and screening tools
- triage systems
- patient-facing AI with action-shaping outputs
- clinical copilots that may influence judgment
