# Case: Ambient Scribe and "Patient-Centered" Claims

Version: `v0.1`

## Public claim pattern

An ambient scribe company describes its product as:

- patient-centered
- reducing clinician burden
- improving the visit experience

## Why this matters

This is a strong example of language compression.

Several different ideas are being folded into one reassuring phrase:

- clinician efficiency
- documentation automation
- workflow redesign
- patient benefit

Those are related, but they are not the same.

## Main editorial question

Is workflow efficiency being accurately described, or rhetorically upgraded into patient benefit?

## Questions to ask

1. Who is the direct user of the system?
2. What exactly improves for the patient?
3. Is there evidence about visit quality, patient comprehension, or outcomes?
4. Does the product only document, or does it also shape action items, coding, referrals, or follow-up?
5. Is "patient-centered" doing more rhetorical work than the visible evidence can support?

## Likely tensions

- A system can reduce clinician burden without clearly improving patient outcomes.
- A system can improve visit flow while still making the patient more invisible in the record workflow.
- A documentation tool can quietly become an action-shaping system.

## What remains unknown

Without stronger public evidence, it is usually unclear:

- whether patient benefit has been measured directly
- whether the system changes downstream decisions
- whether the patient-centered framing is narrow, fair, or overstated

## Useful editorial outcome label

Useful-looking, but the patient-benefit claim may be broader than the visible evidence.
